De-smoothing Technique

De-smoothing is a statistical adjustment applied to private equity returns to correct for artificial smoothing caused by appraisal-based valuations rather than market transactions.

Why It's Needed

  • Infrequent valuations: PE funds typically value assets quarterly or less frequently, not continuously like public markets
  • Appraisal methodology: Valuations are based on models and comparable transactions, not actual market prices
  • Smoothing bias: Appraisers tend to avoid large changes, creating artificially smooth return series
  • Risk understatement: Reported volatility can be 50-70% lower than true economic volatility

How It Works

The de-smoothing technique removes serial correlation from the return series. Serial correlation means that each reported return is partially influenced by previous returns, creating the smoothing effect.

Mathematical Approach

The basic model assumes reported returns follow:

$$r_{\text{reported},t} = \theta \cdot r_{\text{true},t} + (1-\theta) \cdot r_{\text{reported},t-1}$$

Where:

  • $\theta$ (theta) is the smoothing parameter (0 to 1)
  • $r_{\text{true},t}$ is the actual economic return
  • $r_{\text{reported},t}$ is the observed reported return

To recover true returns, we solve for $r_{\text{true},t}$ by estimating $\theta$ from the data using maximum likelihood or regression methods.

Impact on Analysis

After de-smoothing, typical changes include:

  • Volatility increases by 50-100% on average
  • Correlations with public markets increase from 0.3-0.4 to 0.6-0.8
  • Sharpe ratios decline as risk-adjusted returns normalize
  • Beta to equity markets increases significantly
