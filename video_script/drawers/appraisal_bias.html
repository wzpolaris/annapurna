Appraisal Bias in Private Equity

Appraisal bias refers to systematic distortions in private equity valuations that arise because valuations are based on appraisals rather than actual market transactions.

Sources of Bias

  • Stale pricing: Valuations don't update as frequently as market prices, leading to lagged responses to market changes
  • Smoothing behavior: Appraisers tend to avoid large value changes, preferring gradual adjustments
  • Model-based values: Use of DCF or comparable company multiples introduces estimation error
  • Information lag: Appraisals reflect information available at quarter-end, not real-time market conditions
  • Incentive effects: Fund managers may have incentives to report stable values for marketing purposes

Impact on Reported Metrics

Appraisal bias affects several key performance metrics:

  • Volatility: Understated by 50-70%, making PE appear less risky than it truly is
  • Sharpe ratio: Artificially inflated, overstating risk-adjusted performance
  • Correlation: Lower correlation with public markets than economic reality
  • Beta: Understated exposure to market risk factors
  • Drawdowns: Appear smaller and more delayed than actual economic losses

Implications for Investors

Investors using reported PE returns without adjustments may:

  • Overallocate to PE in portfolio optimization
  • Underestimate total portfolio risk
  • Incorrectly assess diversification benefits
  • Misinterpret performance during market crises
  • Set inappropriate risk limits and triggers

Corrections and Adjustments

Professional investors typically apply one or more correction techniques:

  • De-smoothing: Statistical techniques to remove serial correlation
  • Lag adjustments: Adjusting PE returns to account for reporting lag
  • Marked-to-market proxies: Using public market equivalents
  • Cash flow analysis: Analyzing actual distributions rather than NAV changes
