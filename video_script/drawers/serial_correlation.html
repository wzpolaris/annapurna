Serial Correlation in PE Returns

Serial correlation (also called autocorrelation) occurs when a return in one period is correlated with returns in previous periods. In private equity, this is a symptom of smoothing rather than a natural economic phenomenon.

What It Means

In efficient markets with continuous trading (like public equities), returns should be approximately uncorrelated over timeâ€”knowing yesterday's return tells you nothing about today's return. This is a fundamental property of market efficiency.

However, PE returns often show high positive serial correlation, meaning high returns tend to be followed by more high returns, and low returns by more low returns. This pattern indicates smoothing, not predictability.

Measuring Serial Correlation

The first-order autocorrelation coefficient is calculated as:

$$\rho_1 = \text{Corr}(r_t, r_{t-1})$$
  • Typical public equity funds: $\rho_1 \approx 0$ (no correlation)
  • Typical PE funds: $\rho_1 = 0.3$ to $0.7$ (strong positive correlation)

Why It Matters

  • Volatility understatement: Serial correlation mechanically reduces measured volatility
  • Spurious diversification: Low correlation with public markets is partially an artifact
  • Misleading Sharpe ratios: Returns appear smoother and more attractive than reality
  • Portfolio risk: True contribution to portfolio risk is understated

De-smoothing Connection

De-smoothing techniques work by identifying and removing the serial correlation component from reported returns. The stronger the serial correlation, the more aggressive the de-smoothing adjustment needs to be.
